Compensation Drivers in STEM Careers

R Regression Analysis Statistical Modeling
💰

Overview

Investigated compensation patterns across 62,000+ STEM salary records to understand what influences earnings in technical careers. Using multivariate and log-linear regression modeling in R, this study explores relationships between education, experience, career mobility, and compensation outcomes.

The analysis reveals that education level and career mobility significantly impact earnings, while tenure shows limited long-term value in predicting compensation growth.

Key Findings

📚
Education Matters
Advanced degrees correlate with significantly higher compensation across STEM fields
🚀
Mobility Drives Growth
Career mobility (job changes) shows stronger correlation with earnings than tenure at single employer
⏱️
Tenure Has Limits
Years at one company showed diminishing returns on compensation over time

Technical Approach

1. Data Collection & Preparation

  • Analyzed 62,000+ salary records from STEM professionals
  • Cleaned and standardized compensation data across industries
  • Created derived variables for experience, mobility, and career progression

2. Statistical Modeling

  • Built multivariate regression models to identify compensation drivers
  • Applied log-linear transformations to handle skewed salary distributions
  • Tested for statistical significance of education, experience, and mobility factors

3. Analysis & Insights

  • Quantified impact of education level on earning potential
  • Measured correlation between career moves and compensation growth
  • Identified diminishing returns of tenure at single employer

Tools & Technologies

Career Insights

This analysis provides actionable guidance for STEM professionals:

  • Invest in advanced education for long-term earning potential
  • Consider strategic career moves over extended tenure at single employer
  • Focus on skill development and market positioning
  • Understand that loyalty alone may not maximize compensation growth
